# 04 — Deck Copy Standard Note

## Purpose of this document
This document defines the writing standards for the final Project RANGMANCH pitch deck.

Its purpose is to ensure that every slide sounds:
- sharp
- concise
- concrete
- founder-grade
- and easy to understand quickly

---

## Core principle
A strong deck does not win by sounding grand.
It wins by sounding:
- clear
- specific
- intelligent
- and controlled

Project RANGMANCH should never sound like:
- a classroom assignment
- a jargon-heavy AI concept note
- or a vague innovation pitch

It should sound like:
- a product proposal
- from someone who understands
  - users
  - experience design
  - growth
  - business logic
  - and risk

---

# Deck writing rules

## Rule 1 — One idea per slide
Each slide should have one primary job.

Examples:
- explain the insight
- explain the gap
- define the product
- explain growth loops
- explain moat
- explain risks

A slide that tries to do too much becomes forgettable.

---

## Rule 2 — Executive clarity
Write in short, clear, concrete statements.

Prefer:
- “RANGMANCH turns real-life moments into portable entertainment.”
over
- “RANGMANCH is an advanced AI-native interactive entertainment paradigm with highly differentiated multimodal potential.”

The second sounds inflated.
The first sounds sharp.

---

## Rule 3 — Use concrete language
Avoid vague words unless they are immediately supported.

Weak:
- amazing
- engaging
- revolutionary
- immersive
- innovative

Better:
- portable
- continuity-aware
- screenshot-worthy
- short-session
- companion-led
- socially recognizable
- growth-first

Concrete language improves trust.

---

## Rule 4 — Tie claims back to the product
Every major claim in the deck should connect to:
- a visible app surface
- a product loop
- a user behavior
- or a tested insight

Examples:
- shareability → Artifact / Squad Result
- retention → Home / Next Act / Archive
- first wow → Episode Experience
- differentiation → non-chat-generic interface and staged structure

If a claim cannot be tied back to the product, it is too abstract.

---

## Rule 5 — Confident restraint
Do not oversell.

RANGMANCH should sound:
- ambitious
- but grounded
- confident
- but not theatrical
- founder-like
- but not exaggerated

Good tone:
- “The MVP already proves the wedge.”
Bad tone:
- “This will obviously become the future of AI entertainment.”

---

## Rule 6 — Prioritize memorability
Each slide should leave the reviewer with one memorable takeaway.

Examples:
- “This is not a chatbot. It is a companion-led entertainment system.”
- “Artifact is the growth surface.”
- “Continuity is the anti-novelty layer.”
- “Squad Mode is the social wedge.”

Those lines are more valuable than long descriptive paragraphs.

---

## Rule 7 — Avoid heavy jargon
Do not rely on AI or product jargon where simpler language is stronger.

Avoid overusing:
- AI-native
- multimodal
- generative interaction paradigm
- consumer engagement ecosystem
- sticky flywheel infrastructure

Use them only if needed and only where they actually add precision.

---

## Rule 8 — Keep the reviewer moving
A slide should be understandable in a few seconds.

That means:
- short bullets
- strong subheads
- low visual clutter
- minimal dense text
- clear progression from one slide to the next

---

# Writing model for slide content

Each slide should ideally contain:

## A. One strong title
This should state the point of the slide, not merely the topic.

Weak:
- “Growth”
Better:
- “RANGMANCH grows through portable artifacts and social reaction loops.”

## B. One concise support block
This explains the title with 2–4 sharp points.

## C. Optional proof layer
This may be:
- a screenshot
- a product loop
- a table
- a key metric
- a user behavior insight

---

# Tone model for the deck

The deck voice should feel:
- smart
- product-minded
- restrained
- strategic
- and human-readable

It should not feel:
- over-academic
- over-technical
- or over-marketing-driven

---

# Slide-copy anti-patterns to avoid

## Anti-pattern 1 — Paragraph slides
These reduce clarity and reviewer attention.

## Anti-pattern 2 — Buzzword stacking
This weakens trust immediately.

## Anti-pattern 3 — Generic praise
Do not describe the product as:
- fun
- engaging
- innovative
unless you show exactly why.

## Anti-pattern 4 — Repeating the same point across many slides
Every slide must advance the story.

## Anti-pattern 5 — Detaching business language from the product
The deck should not sound like a generic startup template.

---
