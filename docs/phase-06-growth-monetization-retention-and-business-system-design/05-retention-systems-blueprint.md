# 05 — Retention Systems Blueprint

## Purpose of this document
This document defines why users return to Project RANGMANCH after novelty.

Its purpose is to explain:
- what the product-native retention engines are
- what user motivations they serve
- which product surfaces carry them
- what the MVP already proves
- and what future versions deepen later

---

## Core retention principle
Retention should not depend on:
- generic daily reminders
- or vague “habit building” language

Retention should come from specific product-native reasons to return.

RANGMANCH should retain users through:
- curiosity
- continuity
- social evolution
- occasion relevance
- and personalized familiarity

---

# Retention Engine 1 — Daily episode curiosity

## Core user question
“What did my companion create for my mood or moment today?”

## Why it works
This engine supports frequent short-session return behavior.
It turns the product into a repeatable entertainment ritual.

## Product surfaces
- home
- episode setup
- continuity teaser
- onboarding framing of the universe

## What the MVP already proves
- Today’s Episode exists
- the product can generate compact entertainment quickly
- the home screen already points users back into the core loop

## Future expansion
- more dynamic daily tone shifts
- occasion-aware prompts
- richer daily episode variation
- season-based daily continuity

---

# Retention Engine 2 — Arc continuation

## Core user question
“What happens next in my season or current thread?”

## Why it works
This is the engine that prevents the app from feeling like a one-off trick.

## Product surfaces
- next act
- archive
- home continuity card

## What the MVP already proves
- a return hook exists
- continuity is visible after use
- archive stores traces and reinforces persistence

## Future expansion
- deeper arc logic
- multi-episode continuity
- named seasons
- explicit episode chains
- stronger callbacks

---

# Retention Engine 3 — Squad evolution

## Core user question
“How did my group dynamic evolve?”

## Why it works
Social entertainment becomes more powerful when users believe the group state can deepen over time.

## Product surfaces
- squad mode
- squad result
- archive
- next act

## What the MVP already proves
- Squad Mode exists
- group roles and verdicts can be generated
- social outputs produce memorable group framing

## Future expansion
- recurring squad roles
- rivalry or alliance arcs
- shared squad continuity
- multi-session squad memory

---

# Retention Engine 4 — Occasion-triggered return

## Core user question
“What is active today because of context, time, or event?”

## Why it works
Some of the strongest return behavior comes from timely relevance, not just generic daily habit.

## Product surfaces
- event card
- future home transformation
- occasion prompts
- seasonal entry points

## What the MVP already proves
- the app already supports mood and context framing
- the business case can show occasion expansion even if the MVP is still light

## Future expansion
- birthday mode
- festival mode
- sports-night mode
- trip mode
- weekend transforms

---

# Retention Engine 5 — Personalized familiarity

## Core user question
“This app knows my vibe and gets more interesting with me.”

## Why it works
Users return when a product feels progressively more “theirs.”

## Product surfaces
- home profile seed
- continuity callbacks
- companion tone memory
- remembered artifact and episode traces

## What the MVP already proves
- nickname persistence
- vibe persistence
- tone persistence
- recent state reflection on Home, Next Act, and Archive

## Future expansion
- stronger callbacks
- recurring style patterns
- richer profile memory
- stronger season identity

# Why multiple retention engines matter
A weak product often depends on one fragile return reason.

RANGMANCH is stronger because it can eventually stack multiple return reasons:
- personal curiosity
- continuity curiosity
- social curiosity
- event relevance
- profile familiarity

This layered structure is much more believable.

# Example return language

## Daily episode curiosity examples
- “Your next act is ready.”
- “Today’s mood has enough chaos to deserve staging.”
- “Your companion has a fresh interpretation of today.”

## Arc continuation examples
- “Your last act did not end cleanly.”
- “A thread from your previous episode is still active.”
- “The universe has not finished with that outcome yet.”

## Squad evolution examples
- “Your squad’s roles may have shifted.”
- “The group verdict is not fully settled yet.”
- “A social continuation is now active.”

## Occasion-triggered examples
- “Tonight’s moment deserves a special mode.”
- “The universe has detected event-level energy.”
- “This occasion has opened a new act.”

## Personalized familiarity examples
- “Your companion is now tuned to your usual chaos.”
- “Your world is beginning to develop a pattern.”
- “The universe remembers how you usually enter.”

---

# Retention risks

## Risk 1 — Hooks feel fake
Mitigation:
- tie hooks to stored state where possible
- keep them specific enough to feel earned

## Risk 2 — Continuity feels too shallow
Mitigation:
- improve archive visibility
- reinforce home continuity card
- deepen language consistency

## Risk 3 — Product feels like one-off novelty
Mitigation:
- keep return surfaces visible
- show remembered traces
- strengthen next-act framing

---

# Final summary
RANGMANCH should retain users through a layered return system:
- daily episode curiosity
- arc continuation
- squad evolution
- occasion relevance
- personalized familiarity

That layered design is what turns a clever MVP into a believable consumer product.
