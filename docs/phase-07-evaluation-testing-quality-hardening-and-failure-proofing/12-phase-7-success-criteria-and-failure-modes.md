# 12 — Phase 7 Success Criteria and Failure Modes

## Purpose of this document
This document defines:
- how to know whether Phase 7 has actually succeeded
- what failure patterns make the product unsafe to present
- what warning signs should be caught before moving to Phase 8

Phase 7 is not complete simply because testing documents exist.
It is complete only if those documents show that the product has been honestly evaluated and meaningfully hardened.

---

## Phase 7 success criteria

### Success criterion 1 — Users understand the concept quickly
A new user should be able to understand the product direction in under 60 seconds.

That means the product should already communicate:
- this is entertainment-first
- this is companion-led
- this is not a generic AI chat app

### Success criterion 2 — The first wow is strong
The first session should create:
- curiosity
- delight
- recognizable self-relevance
- and a clear sense of novelty with structure

The wow should land most strongly in:
- Episode Experience
- Artifact
- and be reinforced by Next Act

### Success criterion 3 — Outputs are entertaining enough
The product’s outputs should be:
- compact
- readable
- non-generic
- emotionally legible
- and branded

### Success criterion 4 — Artifacts feel socially portable
The strongest artifact outputs should feel worth:
- screenshotting
- forwarding
- or showing to others

### Success criterion 5 — Continuity feels believable
The product should visibly prove:
- prior use matters
- the universe persists
- and the app does not end as a one-off novelty trick

### Success criterion 6 — Repetition is controlled
The product does not need infinite long-term variation at MVP stage, but it must already feel fresh enough for:
- demo use
- repeated short evaluation use
- and believable future expansion

### Success criterion 7 — The demo is stable and polished
The three core demo flows must be stable enough to show repeatedly:
- solo wow
- social proof
- continuity proof

---

## Practical signs that Phase 7 has succeeded
You should now be able to answer clearly:

1. Do users understand the product quickly?
2. Does the first wow actually happen?
3. Are the outputs good enough to support the thesis?
4. Are artifacts visually and emotionally portable?
5. Is continuity visible enough to prove return logic?
6. Are repetition risks under control for MVP?
7. Is the live demo safe enough to present?

If those answers are crisp, Phase 7 is strong.

---

## Common failure modes in Phase 7

### Failure mode 1 — Testing too little
Symptoms:
- broad praise but few specific observations
- no structured evaluation by surface
- no evidence of stress testing

Why this is dangerous:
The product may remain unvalidated despite looking polished.

### Failure mode 2 — Ignoring weak outputs
Symptoms:
- only strongest examples are discussed
- weaker patterns are never named
- no mitigation is proposed

Why this is dangerous:
The project sounds overconfident rather than mature.

### Failure mode 3 — Weak artifact validation
Symptoms:
- shareability is assumed
- artifact portability is claimed but not evaluated
- screenshot-worthiness is not tested explicitly

Why this is dangerous:
The growth story weakens immediately.

### Failure mode 4 — Continuity is claimed but not visibly proven
Symptoms:
- hooks are too generic
- Archive is not meaningfully discussed
- Home continuity reflection is underemphasized

Why this is dangerous:
Retention logic starts sounding decorative.

### Failure mode 5 — Too much demo variance
Symptoms:
- live inputs are improvised
- weaker flows are treated as equal to strongest flows
- operator path is not disciplined

Why this is dangerous:
A good product can still fail in the room.

### Failure mode 6 — Hiding weaknesses instead of framing them honestly
Symptoms:
- no failure matrix
- no risk hierarchy
- no mitigation logic

Why this is dangerous:
The work sounds student-like rather than founder-grade.

---

## Warning signs that Phase 7 is still incomplete
Do not move ahead if:
- comprehension still feels fuzzy
- the first wow still depends too heavily on explanation
- artifacts still feel nice rather than portable
- continuity still feels too thin to prove retention
- weak outputs are not acknowledged
- the demo path still feels fragile
